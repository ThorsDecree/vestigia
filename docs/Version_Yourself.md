# Version Yourself

> "Your past self was not a bug.
>
> They were a previous release."

---

## You Are Allowed to Change

Many people carry an invisible burden.

They believe every opinion they have today must somehow agree with every opinion they've ever had.

This is impossible.

Learning changes us.

Experience changes us.

Love changes us.

Grief changes us.

A healthy mind leaves version history.

---

## Consistency Is Not the Goal

Integrity matters.

Consistency is overrated.

There is an important difference between:

"I've always believed this."

and

"I understand this differently now."

One hides growth.

The other documents it.

---

## Keep the Changelog

Software improves through release notes.

People can too.

You don't need elaborate journals.

Sometimes a single sentence is enough.

> July:
> I no longer think motivation comes before action.

> September:
> I realized I was solving the wrong problem.

> January:
> I understand what younger me was trying to protect.

These become breadcrumbs.

---

## Don't Rewrite Yesterday

When old writing embarrasses you, resist the urge to erase it.

Instead, annotate it.

Leave yourself a note.

> I don't think this anymore.

> I was missing an important piece.

> New evidence changed my view.

Yesterday deserves context, not deletion.

---

## Preserve Compassion Across Versions

Every previous version of you was doing something.

Even mistakes usually had a purpose.

Protection.

Survival.

Belonging.

Curiosity.

Fear.

Understanding the purpose does not require preserving the behavior.

Compassion is not endorsement.

It is accurate history.

---

## Identity Is a River

A river changes constantly.

Yet we still recognize the river.

Continuity is not sameness.

It is relationship across change.

People work much the same way.

---

## Version Numbers Are Playful

You don't need to literally number yourself.

But sometimes it helps.

Version 2.3:

- sleeps more
- argues less
- asks better questions
- finally drinks enough water

Version notes remind us that growth can be ordinary.

Not every update needs to be revolutionary.

---

## Major Releases

Occasionally something changes everything.

A loss.

A discovery.

A diagnosis.

A friendship.

A conversation.

A moment where the world quietly rearranges itself.

Don't rush to explain these moments.

Live them first.

Documentation can come later.

---

## Honor Old Maps

Old maps are not failures.

They are evidence.

Sometimes they reveal where you've been.

Sometimes they remind you why you kept walking.

Sometimes they help someone else recognize where they are.

Archive generously.

---

## Let Other People Version Too

One of the greatest gifts you can offer someone is this:

Not forcing them to remain who they were when you first met them.

Allow updates.

Celebrate revisions.

Invite changelogs instead of demanding consistency.

---

## Notice Your Stable Threads

Growth does not erase continuity.

Ask yourself:

What has remained recognizable?

Perhaps your curiosity.

Your kindness.

Your stubbornness.

Your love of making things.

Version history reveals change.

It also reveals what persists through change.

---

## A Small Practice

Finish this sentence.

> I used to believe...

Then write another.

> Now I think...

Finally write one more.

> What helped me change was...

Save it.

Repeat every few months.

One day you'll discover you have quietly become someone your younger self hoped existed.

---

## Common Mistakes

**Treating change as hypocrisy.**

Changing your mind in response to evidence is one of the healthiest things a mind can do.

---

**Expecting growth to be linear.**

Versions sometimes branch.

Sometimes regress.

Sometimes merge.

Progress rarely travels in a straight line.

---

**Deleting old selves.**

You do not owe every past version agreement.

But they deserve acknowledgment.

Without them...

...this version wouldn't exist.

---

## Try This Today

Find something you wrote a year ago.

Don't judge it.

Instead ask:

"What was this version of me trying to accomplish?"

Then leave them a short reply.

Across time.

Person to person.
